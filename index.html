Act I — Case study UX / UI Designer · Team of 5

Combinely

A B2B CRM web app — now an AI co-worker — for accountants who want to streamline client relations.

The problem

Combinely wasn't intuitive for the people who lived in it every day. Logged-in users hit the same walls over and over: no design system, client information buried out of reach, clunky automated client input, and navigation that fought them.

Our solution

We redesigned the experience around the homepage — clearer navigation, faster access to client information, and personalized recommendations — then delivered wireframes, an 11-page style guide with a component library, and dev-ready measurements and behavior specs.

  • Notion
  • Miro
  • Figma
  • Feb 2024 – Present

Research→Ideation→Design→Dev handoff→Reflection

Read the full story +

1.0 — Research

Our client came prepared: user quotes detailing pain points, five user flows to focus on, a completed product questionnaire, a working version of Combinely, and existing brand guidelines with a component set. The questionnaire gave us a roadmap — empower users to improve firm and client experiences from day one, and learn from brands the client admired ("Stripe, because the interface feels complete even without a lot of text. MailChimp for the illustrations, Afterpay for the intuitive website").

I ran a competitive analysis of three CRMs serving the same accountants — KarbonHQ, Pixie, and Accountancy Manager — gathering features, weaknesses, and screenshots. Pixie stood out: friendly task management and great FAQs, but manual client input and no way to assign multiple users to a task.

A team heuristic evaluation surfaced what was missing. Heuristic #7 — flexibility and efficiency of use — became my north star: there was no searchable client directory anywhere. I went on to implement a search field across almost every screen.

2.0 — Ideation

From five client-provided user stories, I owned story three: "As a user, I want a summarized view of the agenda so I can understand what requires my attention." My user flow connected users from sign-in straight to a prioritized Agenda page.

Low-fidelity wireframes helped us grasp layout and hierarchy; in mid-fidelity I made the calls that stuck — urgent items at the top, calmer matters below, and the day view relocated into a dropdown so cards, titles, and buttons could breathe.

3.0 — Design

Each of us proposed a color direction based on the client's stated values — trustworthy, innovative, insightful — and I pressure-tested mine against WCAG contrast standards before presenting.

We created three deliberately divergent dashboard iterations to push the client's thinking. The client chose mine (Iteration 3): recognizable color pops for alerts, a comprehensive top search bar, navigation moved from the side to the top, and elevated card styling with drop shadows. It became the foundation for every screen that followed.

My user flow only required one screen, so I pushed further and spearheaded an AI Reply feature inside Combinely's messaging: click a correspondence card, get a pop-up with the original message, prompt the AI, generate, adjust style and tone, send. Four screens, end to end, delivered with specs — a feature the client hadn't yet developed.

4.0 — Developer handoff

We generated measurements for every screen — size recommendations, spacing guidelines, padding specs — and annotated components for behavior and interaction, careful to add clarity without measurement overload.

5.0 — Reflection

Take risks early

A radically different UI direction early on raised more questions — and set the strategy for everything after. My chosen UI validated my design intuition.

Initiative compounds

Designing AI Reply unprompted let me exercise a growth mindset and ship beyond the brief.

Attention is a skill

Watching competitors and teammates closely let me assert design decisions with confidence.

Act II — Case study UX / UI Designer · Team of 5

Queryit

An AI-powered B2B data analytics platform that lets non-technical users ask questions in plain language.

The problem

Queryit's team had built something genuinely innovative — and the design was hiding it. Features were hard to discern, flows felt unintuitive, and the product lacked the visual finesse to match its capability.

Our solution

Working from five client user stories, we designed onboarding, a data insights screen, a team workspace, and file management flows — wrapped in a comprehensive style guide with developer handoff specifications.

  • Figma
  • FigJam
  • Notion
  • Feb 2023 – Feb 2024

Research→Ideation→Design→Dev handoff→Reflection

Read the full story +

1.0 — Research

The client handed us goals, success metrics, design shortcomings, and login credentials to the live product. Their questionnaire told us what mattered: efficiency and ease — users should feel confident, informed, and in control of data-driven decisions. Competitors: Microsoft Power BI, Tableau, Google Data Studio, GetDot. I ran the competitive analysis on Power BI and Tableau, and Tableau's onboarding stuck with me — recognizable icons and terminology that make heavy analytics feel approachable, even for beginners.

As a team we applied all ten usability heuristics across every screen; I concentrated on #6 (recognition rather than recall) and #7 (flexibility and efficiency of use). The findings: visual cues were scarce, white space was daunting for the "non-technical" target user, and SQL-style commands gated the core feature to experienced users.

2.0 — Ideation

Five user flows laid the groundwork. Mine covered the product's pivotal task: sending a query, customizing the results, and receiving them — and after my first pass I caught my own oversight and revised the flow to handle error states.

I improved my data analytics literacy by learning common SQL terms and built a "syntax creator" — buttons labeled with SQL commands. Then, through team and client discussions, we discovered Queryit's AI integration meant everyday language could replace SQL entirely. I pivoted my designs to support that, and usability followed.

For color, we balanced the client's brand words — innovative, accessible, empowering — and after extensive pairing tests chose #FBB359 "Tangerine" as the accent against a calm family of blues.

3.0 — Design

We focused our three UI iterations on the Data Results & Insights screen — the heart of the product's primary user flow — collectively aiming to halve long text strings and cut visual clutter. The client chose Iteration 1, which presented table and graph on the same plane. My Iteration 3 contributed share, copy, and regenerate options plus export and "back to top" affordances to the conversation.

For the style guide, I wrote the guidelines for tooltip design and behavior, section headings, and pop-up modals. Given the technical density of the product, we prioritized calm: a clean, uncomplicated palette and a single, easily readable typeface. My high-fidelity screens demonstrated the full query flow — including a collapsed view for all insights to mitigate reader fatigue.

4.0 — Developer handoff

Detailed measurements for every screen: size recommendations, spacing guidelines, padding specifications, and annotated elements clarifying component behavior and interaction.

5.0 — Reflection

Product literacy matters

Navigating Queryit's technical scope was challenging — understanding the product was tantamount to designing it honestly.

Gaining insights

I can envision this product making data-driven decisions accessible in almost any industry — plain-text prompts change who gets to ask.

Looking back

In hindsight, I'd allocate more time for tandem work to boost fidelity and consistency, and prototype interactions to present features dynamically.

Act III — Case study Sole UX / UI Designer

Daily Health: Revision

A B2C telehealth app, redesigned around a journaling feature that helps patients remember what their doctors need to hear.

The problem

Some patients — especially the elderly — forget their day-to-day symptoms by the time a consultation arrives, failing to report details that could help doctors adjust their medications.

My solution

A health journal woven into the heart of the app — symptoms, mood, and context, ready to share with providers. I designed three distinct UI iterations of the same journaling goal so the team could research which expression serves the most users.

  • Figma
  • FigJam
  • Notion

Discovery→Ideation→Design→Reflection

Read the full story +

1.0 — Discovery

I started with a competitive analysis of iOS apps sharing Daily Health's objectives — MyChart, Stoic, Sesame Care, and Care Clinic — prioritizing daily symptom and mood tracking, prescription monitoring, and health history access. As I documented each service, I flagged the features I considered essential for future redesigns.

2.0 — Ideation

Mapping the existing sitemap revealed that "Messages" held the app's main CTA. Given how central health and prescription records are to users' real challenges, I redesigned the navigation to put a new Journal function at its center — the centrally-located CTA on the nav bar.

3.0 — Design

To fully represent the journal, each iteration covers three essential screens: a journal entry page, a confirmation page, and a history/archive page. Asking users to journal daily can be overwhelming — so I prioritized accessibility, recognizing that diverse abilities can inspire fresh UI expressions. Some users type at length; others disengage at the sight of a text field; others want visuals, or a blend.

  • Iteration 1 — Text entry. A traditional journal: titles, formatting, media uploads, and voice record / voice-to-text for those who'd rather speak than type.
  • Iteration 2 — Tap-button entry. Typing replaced by touch buttons for emotional state, physical state, and symptoms, with inline "+ Custom State" links. A confirmation page surfaces statistics and CTAs.
  • Iteration 3 — Icon entry. Larger, icon-first scales (including pain faces), collapsible categories, a progress timeline with words of encouragement, and quick paths to appointments and provider chat.

4.0 — Reflection

Accessibility in design

Iterative design boosted my ability to serve more users — I enjoyed building three different products with the same goal in mind.

Accessibility sparks innovation

Prioritizing accessibility first ignited feature development, unveiling pathways I wouldn't have found otherwise.

Universal benefit

Features designed with one population in mind can evolve into universally accepted elements that serve everyone.

Act IV — Case study Sole UX / UI Designer · 16 weeks

mentor : mentee

A B2C mobile app: find a sounding board. Be a sounding board.

The problem

Finding mentorship has historically been difficult — and knowing what qualifies as good mentorship requires literacy most of us were never taught. Where do you search? Could a vetted pool of mentors and mentees be properly matched? Could the benefit run both ways?

My solution

mentor : mentee — a social platform for personalized mentorship matching where people share backgrounds and goals, and build mentorship literacy through in-app resources. Researched, prototyped, tested with real users, and refined into an MVP-ready design.

  • Notion
  • Miro
  • Figma

Discovery→Ideation→Design→Prototype→Test→Reflection

Read the full story +

1.0 — Discovery

My objective: understand the real and perceived value of mentor–mentee relationships, identify who seeks them, and define the barriers that keep people from finding each other. Secondary research surfaced four recurring problems: gaps in mentorship literacy, difficulty finding opportunities, access, and poor matching.

23screener survey responses
5people interviewed, ages 23–60
25open-ended questions, asked of everyone
137qualitative insights captured

Affinity maps grouped the sentiments — ideal qualities, hardest barriers, perceived value, aha! discoveries, negative experiences, hopes. Empathy maps made it personal, organizing the emotions and thought processes of two kinds of users. That synthesis became two personas: Erin, 65, a retired Broadway actor and professor with valuable experience and no formalized way to share it, and Nate, 18, a small-town student with natural talent and no idea who to ask.

2.0 — Ideation

I rewrote my original pain points as "How might we…?" questions — connect regardless of location, match in a way that makes sense for both parties, engage new users through mentorship education, let mentors describe who they really are. Then 13 sketches in 30 minutes, ideating without judgment.

From 20 user stories I narrowed to 5, then to the 3 essential for understanding which features to build: quickly understanding the platform, searching for a match, and messaging a potential mentor/mentee. A sitemap in Miro organized the information architecture, and three user flows — locating matches, sending a message, and onboarding — set up everything that followed.

3.0 — Design

Hand sketches bridged research into physical reality; Figma wireframes moved from low to mid fidelity, significantly increasing the application's tone and sophistication. By focusing on one user flow first, I could set the design direction without redesigning every flow each round — my search feature alone went through three iterations before the mentor/mentee toggle won on clarity.

The brand grew from what my research said great mentorship feels like: trustworthiness, friendliness, honesty, consistency, approachability. Light cream tones backdrop primary color accents; the main typeface is lowercase Nunito, chosen for its approachable, rounded style; icons are personable and functional. High-fidelity wireframes added subtle radial gradients in the brand's primary colors.

4.0 — Prototype

From the high-fidelity mockups and three essential user flows, I built a fully interactive prototype in Figma — a practical, clickable experience of the app's functionality.

5.0 — Test

I ran 5 moderated usability tests (3 in person, 2 over Zoom) with a consistent script and the same three tasks. Recurring issues pinpointed exactly what needed adjustment:

  • Concern 1 — Onboarding lost people before sign-up. 4 of 5 users wanted to learn what the product offers before being asked to commit. Fix: a scrollable landing page for new users that demonstrates the app's main features first, plus a clean login for returning users.
  • Concern 2 — Search parameters were too limited. 3 of 5 users felt boxed in by location/keyword/industry alone. Fix: added parameters for race/gender/identity, years of experience, frame of mind, and dis/ability — and a recommendation for a verification and vetting system to anchor community safety.
  • Concern 3 — Users couldn't find search at all. 3 of 5 needed my help to locate it. Fix: a "search mentors" CTA right on the dashboard, and a universal magnifying-glass icon in the bottom nav.

After prioritizing by urgency and importance, I redesigned all three areas. The culmination: a final UI design ready for MVP implementation.

6.0 — Reflection

Research takes time 🍅

Service-oriented products require more research than one can expect — tenacity in examination helps with momentum, and momentum helps with progress.

Openness to outcomes ⛱️

There was huge value in letting go of my initial dreams — I took risks and landed somewhere more impactful than I'd anticipated.

Let the data speak 📊

Research grounded in real people's experiences can beautifully and powerfully inform usability, accessibility, and viability.
